Payment rule

At the beginning of the study, the computer has randomly generated a number X between 0 and 100. After you tell us how likely you think the investment is good, say it's Y%, we will compare X and Y.

  • If X>Y, then you will win the bonus of {{ Constants.high_bonus }} with X% chance.
  • If Y>X, then you will win the bonus of {{ Constants.high_bonus }} if the investment you evaluate is indeed good.
In short, you win {{ Constants.high_bonus }} either with X% chance or when the investment is good, whichever you think is more likely.

(In the end, we will tell you the quality of the investment as well as X.)

Why does this (complicated!) rule ensure that you should tell us your true assessment? Imagine you believed that there was a 60% chance that the investment was good. But instead of typing in 60%, you say it's 80%. What happens next?

  • Imagine the number X generated by the computer is 70.
  • Then, since 70 is smaller than 80, the computer will pay you if and only if the investment was good.
This is, of course, a bad deal for you because you now only win with 60% chance according to your true assessment. Had you told us your true assessment and typed in 60%, you would have won with 70% chance!
